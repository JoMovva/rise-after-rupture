This Was Traumatic — Full Stop

An ectopic rupture is both a life-threatening medical emergency and a pregnancy loss at the same time. Research published in the American Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology — one of the largest studies of its kind, following over 650 women — found that nearly 1 in 3 women (29%) met the criteria for post-traumatic stress one month after early pregnancy loss, with women who had ectopic pregnancies showing higher rates of ongoing PTSD than those who had miscarriages.

At 9 months after their loss, 21% of women who had experienced an ectopic pregnancy still showed PTSD symptoms, compared to 16% after miscarriage. What you are carrying is not an overreaction. It is a documented, measurable response to something genuinely traumatic.

The Many Shapes of Grief

Grief after a rupture doesn't come in a neat order or follow a recognizable shape. You may feel all of these, some of these, or things that don't have names yet:

  • Shock and disbelief — a surreal quality to the days after, like it happened to someone else
  • Grief for the baby — for the pregnancy, for the future you had already started to imagine
  • Grief for your body — for the version of yourself who walked into that hospital not knowing what was coming
  • Fear — of hospitals, of being pregnant again, of your own body
  • Guilt — a feeling that you should have known sooner, done something differently (you couldn't have)
  • Anger — at the circumstances, at the randomness of it, sometimes at people who don't understand
  • Relief, and then guilt about the relief — that you survived, that it's over
  • Isolation — a feeling that no one around you truly understands what this was

"There is no right way to grieve. There is no timeline. Some days you will feel strong and some days you will fall apart completely. Both are valid. Both are part of the same healing."

Understanding Post-Traumatic Stress After a Rupture

The Ectopic Pregnancy Trust reports that up to 1 in 3 women may develop distressing symptoms of post-traumatic stress following an ectopic pregnancy. PTSD is not weakness — it is a biological response to a life-threatening event. The four main categories of symptoms to be aware of:

Re-experiencing

  • Flashbacks of the rupture, the ambulance ride, or waking up from surgery
  • Nightmares or vivid, distressing dreams about the event
  • Physical symptoms (racing heart, sweating, nausea) when reminded of what happened

Hypervigilance

  • Feeling constantly on edge or waiting for something else to go wrong
  • Being easily startled, difficulty sleeping, irritability that feels out of proportion
  • Intense anxiety at medical appointments or hospitals

Avoidance and numbness

  • Avoiding hospitals, certain streets, conversations that might touch the experience
  • Emotional numbness — feeling nothing, or feeling disconnected from yourself
  • Difficulty connecting with people who haven't been through this

Negative thoughts and self-blame

  • Persistent guilt or self-blame (this was not your fault)
  • Feeling unsafe in your own body
  • A loss of trust in the future

If these symptoms are significantly affecting your daily life for more than a few weeks, please reach out to a doctor or therapist. Early intervention makes a real difference.

Finding the Right Support

Not all mental health professionals are equally prepared to support survivors of ectopic rupture and pregnancy loss. The most effective evidence-based treatments include:

  • Trauma-focused CBT — cognitive behavioral therapy adapted for PTSD, which helps change how your mind processes traumatic memories
  • EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) — reprocesses distressing memories so they stop causing such intense responses; particularly effective for flashbacks
  • Somatic therapy — body-based approaches, helpful when the physical memory of the rupture feels stored in your body
How to find a grief-informed therapist

Ask your OB for a referral · Search Psychology Today's therapist directory and filter for "grief" and "pregnancy loss" · Look for therapists with experience in perinatal loss or medical trauma · For culturally sensitive support, search for therapists who specifically work with immigrants or who share your cultural background · BetterHelp and Talkspace offer online therapy accessible from anywhere in the US

"Seeking support is not a sign that you aren't coping. It is a sign that you are taking your own suffering seriously — which is exactly what you deserve to do."

Day to Day: What May Help

  • Journaling — give your grief a place to exist without judgment or an audience
  • A small ritual to acknowledge the baby you lost — a plant, a candle, a quiet moment each week
  • Talking to women who have been through it — peer connection is different from professional support, and both matter
  • Limiting the pressure to explain or perform recovery for others who haven't been through it
  • Gentle movement and time outdoors — both help regulate the nervous system over time
  • Naming what you are feeling, even to yourself — language gives grief a shape, and a shape makes it more navigable
